# Mastercard — Cybersecurity Job Simulation

**Platform:** Forage
**Role:** Security Awareness Analyst (simulated)
**Team:** Mastercard Security Awareness Team

## Overview
Phishing is still one of the most common ways organizations get breached — 
not because the technology is sophisticated, but because it targets people, 
not systems. This simulation put me on Mastercard's Security Awareness Team, 
responsible for testing and training employees against exactly that.

## What I did

### 1. Analyzing a phishing sample
Started with a deliberately weak, obviously-fake phishing email and broke 
down why it failed: poor grammar, a mismatched sender address, and no context 
tying it to the target. Real phishing emails succeed by fixing exactly these 
flaws.

### 2. Designing a realistic simulated phishing email
Rewrote the sample into something a real employee could plausibly fall for — 
professional tone, urgency framed around a plausible IT scenario (account 
compromise, password reset), and a disguised link. The goal wasn't to trick 
people maliciously, but to build a training tool that actually tests 
vigilance rather than testing spelling ability.

### 3. Interpreting simulation results
Reviewed simulated campaign data — click-through rates and rates of 
credential submission — broken down by department. Identified which teams 
were most susceptible and looked for patterns (e.g., non-technical teams 
under time pressure being more prone to clicking).

### 4. Building the training response
Put together a department-specific awareness brief: concrete visual examples 
of red flags to look for, clear and non-technical language, and actionable 
steps rather than abstract warnings.

## Key takeaway
Effective phishing defense isn't just spotting bad grammar — it's 
understanding attacker psychology (urgency, authority, fear) well enough to 
simulate it convincingly, so the training actually reflects what a real 
attack looks like. A phishing test that's too obvious teaches nothing.
