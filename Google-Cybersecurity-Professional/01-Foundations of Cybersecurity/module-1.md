# What is Cybersecurity?

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting computers, servers, networks, applications, and digital information from unauthorized access, theft, damage, or disruption.

Its primary goal is to ensure that information remains secure while allowing authorized users to access the resources they need.

Cybersecurity protects both individuals and organizations from cyber attacks that may result in financial loss, data breaches, identity theft, or service disruption.

In today's digital world, almost every organization relies on cybersecurity to protect its operations and maintain customer trust.

---

# Why Cybersecurity is Important

Technology has become an essential part of everyday life. Businesses store customer information, governments maintain public records, hospitals manage patient data, and financial institutions process millions of online transactions every day.

Without proper cybersecurity measures, attackers can exploit vulnerabilities to:

- Steal confidential information
- Interrupt business operations
- Damage an organization's reputation
- Cause financial losses
- Compromise customer privacy

Strong cybersecurity helps organizations maintain the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of their information systems.

# What Does a Security Analyst Do?

A Security Analyst is responsible for protecting an organization's digital assets by monitoring systems, identifying vulnerabilities, responding to incidents, and implementing security controls.

Some common responsibilities include:

- Monitoring network activity
- Investigating security alerts
- Identifying vulnerabilities
- Managing security tools
- Responding to security incidents
- Conducting risk assessments
- Documenting findings
- Supporting compliance requirements

Security Analysts work closely with other IT teams to ensure systems remain secure and resilient against cyber threats.

# The Security Mindset

One of the most important concepts introduced in this module is developing a **security mindset**.

A security mindset means thinking proactively about potential risks before they become security incidents.

Instead of asking:

*"How does this system work?"*

Security professionals also ask:

- What could go wrong?
- How could this system be abused?
- What happens if someone gains unauthorized access?
- What security controls are missing?

This approach helps identify weaknesses before attackers can exploit them.

# Common Cybersecurity Threats

Organizations face many different types of cyber threats.

Some common examples include:

### Malware

Malicious software designed to damage systems or steal information.

Examples include viruses, worms, ransomware, and spyware.

---

### Phishing

Fraudulent emails or messages designed to trick users into revealing passwords or sensitive information.

Phishing remains one of the most common attack methods used by cybercriminals.

---

### Social Engineering

Attackers manipulate human behavior rather than exploiting technical vulnerabilities.

Examples include:

- Fake phone calls
- Impersonation
- Pretexting
- Tailgating

---

### Insider Threats

Not all threats come from external attackers.

Employees, contractors, or trusted users may intentionally or accidentally expose sensitive information.

---

### Password Attacks

Weak or reused passwords make it easier for attackers to gain unauthorized access through methods such as brute-force attacks or credential stuffing.

# Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA)

Although explored in greater detail later in the course, this module briefly introduces the CIA Triad.

### Confidentiality

Only authorized users should access sensitive information.

### Integrity

Data should remain accurate and unchanged unless modified by authorized users.

### Availability

Systems and information should remain accessible whenever authorized users need them.

These three principles form the foundation of modern cybersecurity.

# Ethics in Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity professionals often have access to highly sensitive systems and confidential information.

Because of this responsibility, ethical behavior is essential.

Security professionals must:

- Respect privacy
- Follow organizational policies
- Comply with laws and regulations
- Protect sensitive information
- Report vulnerabilities responsibly

Ethics help build trust between organizations, customers, and security teams.

# Why Continuous Learning Matters

Cybersecurity is constantly evolving.

New vulnerabilities, attack techniques, and defensive technologies appear regularly.

Successful cybersecurity professionals continuously:

- Study new technologies
- Practice hands-on labs
- Earn certifications
- Participate in Capture The Flag (CTF) competitions

- Follow industry news

Continuous learning helps professionals stay prepared for emerging threats.
